# Pride in Place × EFL Community Impact — Synthesis Report

> **Comparing the UK Government's Pride in Place Programme with the EFL's Community Impact (2022–24)**  
> **Date:** April 2026

---

## 1. Purpose of This Report

This report examines the UK Government's **Pride in Place Programme** and the **English Football League's Community Impact Report (2022–24)** side by side. The aim is to identify where these two agendas converge — in objectives, geography, target communities, and delivery mechanisms — and where they diverge in approach, philosophy, or direction. The synthesis highlights strategic opportunities for alignment and flags tensions that could arise if the two frameworks are brought together.

---

## 2. At a Glance — Comparative Overview

| Dimension | Pride in Place Programme | EFL Community Impact |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Lead Body** | UK Government (MHCLG) | English Football League (72 clubs + charities) |
| **Nature** | Government regeneration funding programme | Private/charitable sector community delivery |
| **Total Investment** | £5.8bn over 10 years | £154.67m community investment (2023/24 alone) |
| **Geographic Reach** | ~380 communities across England, Scotland, Wales | 72 clubs across England & Wales |
| **Decision-Making** | Neighbourhood Boards (community-led, LA accountable) | Club Community Organisations (CCOs) and club trusts |
| **Duration** | 10-year endowment-style funding | Ongoing, season-by-season delivery |
| **Primary Focus** | Place-based physical and social regeneration | People-centred activity, health, education, social connection |
| **Core Metric** | Community pride, deprivation indices, cohesion scores | Social value (£1.24bn), participation (1.1m people), activity hours (8.17m) |

## 3. Where They Converge

### 3.1 Shared Strategic Pillars

Both programmes organise their work around strikingly similar themes. The alignment is not coincidental — both are responding to the same underlying challenges in the UK's most deprived communities.

| Pride in Place Pillar | EFL Equivalent | Overlap |
|-----------------------|---------------|---------|
| **Stronger Communities** — belonging, cohesion, resilience, trust | **Social Connections** (£40.12m) & **Social Cohesion** (£2.16m) — 80% of participants reported improved social connections, 71% felt more part of their community | Both aim to rebuild the social fabric in places where trust, identity, and togetherness have eroded |
| **Thriving Places** — high streets, public spaces, community assets | **Economic Development** (£78.71m) & local expenditure (£408.52m in local communities) — clubs sustain 53,518 local jobs | Both drive economic activity in local areas, though PiP focuses on the built environment while EFL drives footfall and spending |
| **Empowering People** — community-led decision-making, civic agency | **Education, Skills & Training** (£494.34m, 238,010 participants) — clubs deliver employability, qualifications, life skills | Both aim to give people agency, though PiP focuses on democratic participation while EFL focuses on personal development pathways |

### 3.2 Overlapping Geographies

This is perhaps the most powerful point of convergence. EFL clubs are overwhelmingly located in **exactly the kinds of towns and cities** that Pride in Place targets:

- **Post-industrial towns** — Burnley, Barnsley, Rotherham, Stoke, Wigan, Port Vale, Doncaster
- **Coastal communities** — Blackpool, Plymouth, Grimsby, Sunderland
- **Northern and Midlands "left behind" areas** — Bradford, Huddersfield, Oldham (via Rochdale/Bolton), Walsall, Shrewsbury
- **South Wales valleys** — Swansea, Cardiff, Newport, Wrexham

EFL clubs are rarely in affluent commuter towns. They are, by their nature, embedded in the communities that Pride in Place is designed to serve. The 72 EFL clubs and their Community Organisations likely overlap with a significant proportion of the ~380 Pride in Place areas.

### 3.3 Clubs as Anchor Institutions

The Pride in Place programme explicitly encourages Neighbourhood Boards to identify and work with **anchor institutions** — trusted, permanent organisations rooted in the local area. The EFL report makes a compelling case that football clubs are precisely this:

> *"Clubs are integral to the social and cultural fabric of their communities"* — EFL Impact Report

> *"It's absolutely vital that we can build places that people can come along, take part, and feel proud of their community."* — EFL Impact Report, p.31

Key evidence:
- **93%** of respondents agreed clubs are important to their local communities
- **90%** believe clubs should provide services to local communities
- **6,000+ staff** already delivering community programmes
- **8.17 million hours** of activity delivered annually
- Clubs operate **year-round**, not just on matchdays

Football clubs hold a unique position that very few other institutions can match: they carry **deep emotional identity** tied to place. A town's football club is often the single most recognisable symbol of that community's identity — precisely the kind of "pride in place" the government programme seeks to reignite.

### 3.4 Health & Wellbeing Outcomes

Both programmes recognise that community regeneration must address health:

| Metric | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Physical Health** | Public spaces, sports facilities, play areas, green spaces | £412.33m social value; 349,546 participants in sport/physical activity |
| **Mental Health** | Stronger communities, belonging, reduced isolation | £210.15m social value; targeted mental health programmes |
| **Social Prescribing** | Boards can commission community health activities | Clubs already deliver NHS-aligned social prescribing, EFL programmes like "Fit Fans" |

The EFL is already delivering at scale in health outcomes. Pride in Place could fund the **infrastructure** (facilities, pitches, community spaces) while EFL clubs provide the **programming and engagement** — a natural partnership.

### 3.5 Education, Employment & Skills

| Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Revenue funding for capacity building and employability | £494.34m social value in education, skills & training |
| Boards can commission training and skills programmes | 238,010 participants in education programmes |
| Targeting economically disadvantaged neighbourhoods | Clubs run NVQs, apprenticeships, mentoring, employability courses |

EFL clubs are already **proven delivery partners** for exactly the education and skills outcomes Pride in Place aims to achieve. Many clubs deliver programmes commissioned by local authorities, DWP, and the NHS — this is not new territory for them.

## 4. Where They Diverge

### 4.1 Hard Infrastructure vs. Soft Intervention

This is the most significant divergence in emphasis:

| | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|-|---------------|---------------------|
| **Primary Investment** | **Built environment** — buildings, streetscapes, public realm, physical assets | **People and programmes** — coaching, courses, sessions, outreach |
| **What Gets Funded** | Bricks, mortar, facades, parks, playgrounds, market infrastructure | Staffing, activity hours, programme delivery, participant engagement |
| **Theory of Change** | Improve the *place* → people feel pride → community strengthens | Engage the *people* → lives improve → community strengthens |

Pride in Place starts with the **place**. The EFL starts with the **person**. Both arrive at the same destination (stronger communities), but the causal logic runs in different directions. A synthesis would recognise that **both are necessary** — you need quality spaces *and* quality programmes to fill them.

### 4.2 Democratic Governance vs. Institutional Delivery

| | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|-|---------------|---------------------|
| **Decision-Making** | Neighbourhood Boards — residents, businesses, community groups vote on priorities | Club boards and Community Organisation trustees decide programming |
| **Accountability** | Local authority as accountable body, MHCLG oversight | Club governance, Charity Commission (for CCOs), EFL oversight |
| **Community Voice** | Mandatory deep, inclusive, ongoing engagement | Community consultation, but ultimately club/charity-led decisions |
| **Power Structure** | Bottom-up by design (community empowerment is a stated objective) | Top-down delivery by benevolent institution to community beneficiaries |

This is a genuine tension. Pride in Place is built on the principle of **community power** — residents deciding their own priorities. EFL community work, while impactful and valued, is typically **delivered to** communities rather than **designed by** them. If EFL clubs want to be serious partners in Pride in Place, they may need to adapt governance models to give communities more direct influence over programme design.

### 4.3 Measurement & Evaluation Philosophy

| | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|-|---------------|---------------------|
| **Approach** | "Light-touch" monitoring; focus on process (board engagement, plan quality, spend forecasts) | Rigorous social value measurement; £1.24bn quantified via SROI-style methodology |
| **Metrics** | Input-focused (community reached, money spent, projects delivered) | Outcome-focused (social value generated, participant wellbeing change, behaviour change) |
| **Maturity** | Early-stage; evaluation framework still developing | Well-established; Substance has been measuring EFL impact for multiple cycles |

The EFL's measurement apparatus is more sophisticated than what Pride in Place currently requires. This is an opportunity — EFL methodology could help Neighbourhood Boards **demonstrate the value** of community investments more effectively, which in turn helps make the case for continued funding.

### 4.4 Scale & Consistency

| | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|-|---------------|---------------------|
| **Consistency** | Highly variable — each Neighbourhood Board sets its own priorities, leading to very different outcomes area by area | Relatively consistent — all 72 clubs deliver across similar programme pillars (health, education, social connection) |
| **National Brand** | Government programme identity; local branding varies | Unified EFL brand with enormous public recognition and emotional resonance |
| **Engagement Reach** | Still establishing — boards in formation, plans being developed | Already reaching 1.1m+ people annually through established channels |

The EFL has an extraordinary advantage in **reach and brand trust**. In many target communities, more people could name their local football club than their local council. This brand recognition and existing engagement infrastructure is something Pride in Place lacks and could benefit enormously from.

### 4.5 Sustainability Models

| | Pride in Place | EFL Community Impact |
|-|---------------|---------------------|
| **Funding Model** | Time-limited (10 years), then expected to have built self-sustaining capacity | Diverse funding mix — club subsidy, grants, commissioned services, earned income |
| **Risk** | "Cliff-edge" after 10 years if communities haven't built sustainable structures | Club financial volatility — relegation, ownership changes, and administration can devastate community programmes |
| **Long-Term Vision** | Transition to community-led bodies (CICs, charities) by Year 3 | CCOs already operate as independent charities, but remain dependent on parent club brand and facilities |

Both face sustainability risks, but of different kinds. Pride in Place faces the classic **programme-end cliff**. EFL community work faces the **club volatility risk** — when a club is relegated or enters administration, community programmes often suffer disproportionately despite being the most socially impactful part of the organisation.

## 5. Strategic Opportunities

### 5.1 EFL Clubs as Pride in Place Delivery Partners

The most obvious synthesis. EFL Community Organisations could:
- Sit as members on **Neighbourhood Boards** (representing community sports/health interests)
- Deliver **contracted programmes** funded by Pride in Place budgets
- Provide **venues and facilities** for community engagement activities
- Act as the **anchor institution** that boards transition governance to (or partner with) by Year 3

### 5.2 Pride in Place Funding for EFL Community Infrastructure

Clubs often operate community programmes from outdated or inadequate facilities. Pride in Place capital funding could:
- Build or refurbish **community hubs** at or near club grounds
- Upgrade **3G pitches, sports halls, and multi-use games areas** in target neighbourhoods
- Fund **high street presence** for community programmes (drop-in centres, skills hubs)

### 5.3 Shared Measurement Frameworks

The EFL's social value methodology (delivered by Substance) could be adopted or adapted by Neighbourhood Boards to:
- Quantify the **social return on investment** for Pride in Place funded projects
- Provide **credible evidence** to MHCLG that investments are delivering outcomes
- Enable **cross-programme comparison** between different types of intervention

### 5.4 Matchday as Community Engagement Platform

EFL clubs attract significant footfall on matchdays — a ready-made audience for:
- **Pride in Place consultations** and community engagement exercises
- **Health screenings**, benefits advice, and skills information
- **Neighbourhood Board recruitment** and awareness-raising
- Creating visible links between the club's identity and the wider pride in place agenda

### 5.5 Joint Approach to Social Cohesion

The March 2026 expansion of Pride in Place specifically targets areas with low social cohesion. Football is one of the most powerful tools for social cohesion in the UK:
- Clubs bring together people across age, ethnicity, and socioeconomic background
- Community tournaments, walking football, disability sport, and women's & girls' programmes actively build bridges
- The EFL reports **80% of participants feel more socially connected** — precisely the outcome the cohesion expansion is trying to achieve

## 6. Tensions & Risks

### 6.1 Democratic Accountability vs. Institutional Control
If EFL clubs become major delivery partners for Pride in Place, there is a risk that community voice is subordinated to institutional priorities. Boards must retain genuine decision-making power, not become rubber stamps for club-led agendas.

### 6.2 Football's Exclusionary Potential
Despite its broad appeal, football is not universally welcoming. Some community members — particularly women, older people, ethnic minorities, and those with no interest in sport — may feel alienated by a football-centric approach. Pride in Place is deliberately pluralistic; over-reliance on one institution could narrow its appeal.

### 6.3 Club Financial Instability
EFL clubs face relegation, ownership changes, and financial distress. Tying long-term community regeneration to institutions with volatile financial foundations carries risk. The independence of Community Organisations (as separate charities) mitigates this partially, but brand and facility access remain tied to the parent club.

### 6.4 Measurement Mismatch
The EFL measures **social value in monetary terms** (£1.24bn). Pride in Place measures **process and engagement**. These are fundamentally different evaluation philosophies. Attempting to force one into the other's framework could distort reporting or create perverse incentives.

### 6.5 Capital vs. Revenue Tension
Pride in Place is weighted towards **capital** spending (buildings, infrastructure). EFL community work is overwhelmingly **revenue** (staff, programmes, delivery). If Partnership is pursued, there's a risk of building beautiful facilities with no funding for the programmes to run inside them.

---

## 7. Conclusions

### The Case for Alignment Is Strong

The overlap between Pride in Place and EFL community work is extensive and natural:
- They serve the **same communities** — post-industrial, coastal, and "left behind" towns
- They share the **same objectives** — community pride, social connection, health, economic vitality
- They offer **complementary strengths** — PiP brings capital funding and democratic governance; EFL brings delivery infrastructure, brand trust, and proven programming
- The EFL's existing **£1.24bn in social value** and **1.1m engaged participants** represent exactly the kind of outcomes Pride in Place is designed to achieve

### But Synthesis Requires Careful Design

The divergences are real. A productive partnership would need to:

1. **Protect community voice** — ensure Neighbourhood Boards retain genuine power and are not captured by institutional partners
2. **Bridge the capital-revenue gap** — ensure funding can support both infrastructure *and* the programmes that bring it to life
3. **Respect pluralism** — football is a powerful vehicle, but not the only one; boards must reflect the full diversity of community interests
4. **Align measurement** — develop shared frameworks that satisfy both MHCLG's light-touch monitoring and the EFL's social value quantification
5. **Manage institutional risk** — build safeguards so that community regeneration is not derailed by club-level financial instability

### The Bigger Picture

At its best, this synthesis represents something genuinely powerful: **the UK's most emotionally resonant local institutions** (football clubs) working hand-in-hand with **the UK's most ambitious community empowerment programme** (Pride in Place) to rebuild pride, agency, and opportunity in the places that need it most.

The EFL report's conclusion puts it well: clubs are *"critical pillars"* of their communities — not just on matchdays, but 365 days a year. Pride in Place offers the long-term funding and democratic framework to harness that potential at a scale never previously attempted.
