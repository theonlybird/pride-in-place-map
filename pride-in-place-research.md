# UK Government Pride in Place Programme — Research Report

> **Last Updated:** April 2026  
> **Status:** Active Programme — Delivery Phase  
> **Lead Department:** Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government (MHCLG)

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The **Pride in Place Programme** is the UK Government's flagship long-term community regeneration initiative. Launched in **September 2025**, it represents a total investment commitment of up to **£5.8 billion over 10 years**, targeting hundreds of the most deprived and socially disconnected neighbourhoods across England, Scotland, and Wales (with equivalent arrangements for Northern Ireland).

Each selected area receives up to **£20 million** in funding and support over a decade, with decisions on how funding is spent led by locally-formed **Neighbourhood Boards** comprising residents, businesses, community organisations, and local leaders.

The programme is the successor to and expansion of the previous government's **Long-Term Plan for Towns** (2023), which was rebranded as the **Plan for Neighbourhoods** (March 2025) before being brought under the current **Pride in Place** umbrella strategy.

## 2. Programme Evolution & Timeline

### 2.1 Historical Context

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **2023** | **Long-Term Plan for Towns** launched by the previous administration, targeting 75 towns with £20 million each over 10 years |
| **March 2025** | Programme rebranded to **Plan for Neighbourhoods** by the new government; scope and objectives refreshed to align with government missions |
| **September 2025** | **Pride in Place Strategy** officially launched — the Plan for Neighbourhoods is absorbed as Phase 1 under the new umbrella programme |
| **2024–2025** | £250,000 capacity funding grants provided to local authorities to establish Neighbourhood Boards and begin community engagement |
| **UK Shared Prosperity Fund (UKSPF)** | The UKSPF — which had included "pride in place" as a priority — scheduled to end March 2026, with the Pride in Place Programme serving as part of the long-term successor framework |

### 2.2 Key Programme Milestones

| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| **September 2025** | Official launch of the Pride in Place Strategy and prospectus |
| **28 November 2025** | Deadline for 75 Phase 1 Neighbourhood Boards to submit their Pride in Place Plans to MHCLG for assessment |
| **April 2026** | Phase 1 delivery funding released to lead local authorities — official start of the delivery phase |
| **April 2026** | 2026–2027 capacity funding payment issued to all participating places |
| **17 July 2026** | Deadline for 169 Phase 2 Neighbourhood Boards to finalise membership and confirm area boundaries |
| **March 2026** | Expansion of 40 additional communities announced under the social cohesion action plan "Protecting What Matters" |
| **Ongoing to 2035** | 10-year delivery period for all funded areas |

## 3. Funding Structure & Allocations

### 3.1 Core Programme Funding

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Total Investment** | Up to **£5.8 billion** over 10 years |
| **Funding per Area** | Up to **£20 million** per neighbourhood over 10 years |
| **Number of Areas** | Approximately **300+ communities** across Great Britain (expanding to ~380 including all strands) |
| **Funding Type** | Mix of **capital** and **revenue** funding |

### 3.2 Phased Rollout

#### Phase 1 — 75 Areas
- Inherited from the original Long-Term Plan for Towns / Plan for Neighbourhoods
- Plans submitted by November 2025
- Delivery funding released from April 2026

#### Phase 2 — 169 Areas
- Expanded roster of communities added under the Pride in Place Programme
- Board membership and boundaries to be finalised by July 2026
- Delivery funding commencing from mid-2026 onwards

#### March 2026 Expansion — 40 Additional Communities
- Announced as part of the **"Protecting What Matters"** social cohesion action plan
- **Additional investment:** £800 million over 10 years
- Each area eligible for up to £20 million
- Selected using a new **"community cohesion filter"** alongside the existing Index of Multiple Deprivation and Community Needs Index
- Specifically targets areas where social cohesion is under the greatest pressure (using the Community Life Survey)

### 3.3 Pride in Place Impact Fund

In addition to the core 10-year programme, a separate **Pride in Place Impact Fund** provides shorter-term, immediate support:

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Total Fund** | **£150 million** |
| **Number of Areas** | **95 local authority areas** |
| **Funding per Area** | **£1.5 million** each |
| **Duration** | 2 years (2025/26 and 2026/27) |
| **Purpose** | Immediate, visible capital projects to restore community pride and revitalise local areas |
| **Deadline** | Projects to be delivered by **March 2027** |
| **Governance** | Local authorities select and deliver projects — no individual project-level approval from central government required |
| **Reporting** | Progress reported via MHCLG digital tool |

### 3.4 Capacity Funding

- **£250,000** grants provided to participating local authorities
- Used to establish Neighbourhood Boards, begin community engagement, and develop Pride in Place Plans
- Allocated annually with a 2026–2027 payment confirmed for all participating places

### 3.5 Eligible Spending Categories

#### Capital Investment
- Refurbishment or repurposing of community buildings (halls, youth centres, cultural venues)
- Upgrading playgrounds, parks, public squares, and green spaces
- High street façade improvements and streetscape enhancements
- New cycleways, footpaths, and shared-use infrastructure
- Infrastructure for public markets and wayfinding

#### Revenue Investment
- Community engagement and consultation activities
- Running Neighbourhood Boards
- Supporting voluntary and community sector activities
- Developing Pride in Place Plans
- Capacity building in early years

#### General Exclusions
- Core ongoing administrative/staffing costs (unless specifically permitted for capacity building)
- Activity that took place prior to the designated start date
- Reclaimable VAT
- Party-political activity, lobbying, or exclusive religious activities
- Revenue expenditure charged against capital-only allocations (and vice versa)

## 4. Locations & Regional Coverage

### 4.1 Geographic Scope

The programme operates across **England, Scotland, and Wales**, with the UK Government working with the Northern Ireland Executive to determine equivalent delivery arrangements.

### 4.2 Selection Methodology

Areas are selected using a composite approach that combines:

| Metric | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD)** | National-level data on deprivation across income, employment, health, education, crime, housing, and living environment |
| **Community Needs Index** | Measures civic infrastructure, connectivity, and community engagement capacity |
| **Community Life Survey (CLS)** | Used specifically for the March 2026 cohesion expansion to identify areas with the lowest social cohesion scores |

The programme targets neighbourhoods identified as **"doubly disadvantaged"** — areas with both high levels of deprivation and weak social infrastructure.

### 4.3 Regional Differences by Nation

#### England
- Largest share of funded communities
- Delivered through MHCLG directly
- Boards expected to align with local authority regeneration strategies and devolution deal priorities

#### Scotland
- Neighbourhood Boards encouraged to align with:
  - Scottish Government's **Regeneration Strategy**
  - **Town Centre First** principle
  - **Town Centre Action Plan**
- Methodology tailored to reflect Scottish data availability (e.g., Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation)

#### Wales
- Boards expected to consider:
  - Welsh Government's **Programme for Government**
  - **Well-being of Future Generations Act**
  - **Transforming Towns** programme
  - **Future Wales: National Plan 2040**
- Some friction reported regarding perceived bypass of devolved regeneration strategies

#### Northern Ireland
- UK Government has committed to working with the Northern Ireland Executive on delivery
- Specific arrangements (e.g., for Belfast) being developed in partnership
- Delivery approach to be determined in collaboration with the devolved administration

## 5. Governance & Delivery Model

### 5.1 Neighbourhood Boards

The centrepiece of the programme's governance is the **Neighbourhood Board**:

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Chair** | Independent Chair |
| **Members** | Residents, local business owners, grassroots campaigners, workplace representatives, faith leaders, community organisations |
| **Supporting Roles** | Local MPs and councillors |
| **Responsibility** | Co-produce a 10-year Pride in Place Plan; decide how funding is spent |
| **Accountability** | Local authorities act as the accountable body |
| **Transparency** | Expected to publish board membership, meeting agendas, and minutes |

### 5.2 Transition to Community-Led Governance

The programme encourages boards to transition toward an independent, community-led delivery model **by Year 3**, such as:
- Community Interest Company (CIC)
- Registered charity
- Partnership with an anchor organisation

### 5.3 Local Authority Role

Local authorities serve as the **accountable body**, responsible for:
- Ensuring funds are distributed fairly and effectively
- Compliance with public sector standards (Nolan Principles, *Managing Public Money*)
- Submitting monitoring returns via the MHCLG digital tool
- Supporting Neighbourhood Boards with administrative and governance capacity

### 5.4 Monitoring & Evaluation

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Approach** | "Light-touch" monitoring to minimise administrative burden |
| **Reporting** | Via MHCLG digital tool |
| **Data Captured** | Capacity funding activities, project delivery, planned projects, board details, forecasted spend |
| **Engagement Tracking** | Activity description, target audience, estimated number of beneficiaries |
| **Support** | Organisations like **Locality** provide tools and guidance on governance, engagement, and monitoring impact |

│  • Resilience        │  │  • Community assets   │  │  • Local control     │

└─────────────────────┘  └─────────────────────┘  └─────────────────────┘

### 6.2 Expected Tangible Outcomes

#### High Streets & Town Centres
- Shop frontage improvements
- Repurposing of abandoned or vacant sites
- Streetscape enhancements (lighting, signage, paving)
- Infrastructure for local markets
- Making town centres more welcoming and economically active

#### Community Assets
- Refurbishment of community facilities (libraries, halls, youth centres)
- Enabling community organisations to take ownership of valued local spaces
- Restoring community gathering points (e.g., community pubs, cultural venues)

#### Public Spaces
- Improvements to parks, public squares, and green spaces
- New or upgraded outdoor play areas
- Sports and leisure facility improvements
- Street furniture, public art, and wayfinding

#### Civic Engagement & Agency
- Establishment of permanent governance structures (Neighbourhood Boards → community-led bodies)
- Residents, businesses, and local leaders co-creating regeneration plans
- Measurable increase in community participation and satisfaction with local area
- Rebuilding trust between communities and institutions

#### Community & Social Outcomes
- Increased sense of belonging and local identity
- Improved safety perceptions
- Stronger community resilience
- Enhanced social cohesion (particularly in the 40 expansion areas)

## 8. Criticisms & Challenges

### 8.1 Governance & Accountability Tensions
- Local authorities carry the accountability burden while boards drive decisions — creating tension between compliance requirements and community-led flexibility
- Risk of mismanagement due to reliance on local volunteers and varied partnerships

### 8.2 Geographic Targeting Concerns
- Questions raised (including by the Joseph Rowntree Foundation) about whether funding alignment truly matches the intensity and geography of need
- Rural areas may be underserved by selection metrics that favour traditional urban deprivation models
- Political scrutiny over whether selections were influenced by the "geography of discontent" rather than pure evidence

### 8.3 Sustainability & Substitution Risk
- Risk that stretched councils may treat Pride in Place funding as a **replacement** for cut core grants, rather than additional regeneration resource
- Long-term sustainability of improvements beyond the 10-year funding period is uncertain
- Lasting success depends on integration with wider social enterprises, health bodies, and anchor institutions

### 8.4 Capacity & Engagement Gaps
- Disadvantaged communities may lack the capacity and resources to effectively form boards and manage complex regeneration plans
- For every engaged community member, many more remain unaware or disengaged
- Uneven distribution of community capacity across the country

### 8.5 Devolution Tensions
- In Wales particularly, the programme has faced criticism for appearing to bypass devolved government regeneration strategies
- Perception of a "Whitehall-led" policy that can feel disconnected from local regional priorities and governance structures

### 8.6 Legacy Programme Concerns
- The programme inherits elements from previous "levelling up" and "towns" initiatives
- Critics argue social infrastructure investment, while positive, is not a substitute for adequately funding local government or investing in national-scale infrastructure
- The classic "centralisation vs. local empowerment" tension remains unresolved
